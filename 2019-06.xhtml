I received a set of birch specimens loaned to Hugh McAllister. I examined these and compared them with the notes he took on the specimens and the provisional key that he wrote. I spent a good part of the day examining specimens. I learned that, in counting the major veins, the smaller secondary veins toward the apex of the leaf are not counted. I also learned to recognize the basal leaves on the stems, which are most useful for diagnosis. The hair on petioles that Hugh refers to is not the hair on the adaxial surface of the petiole. All of the birches I have been examining have fine hair here. There are often hairs on the abaxial surface of petioles and on the young stems on Betula kenaica; these are absent in Betula pendula. I was not able to see useful characters in the catkin scales in the specimens I examined. None of the female catkin scales that I examined had reflexed lateral lobes in B. pendula or B. kenaica. I started entering Hugh's identifications and notes into the corresponding records on Arctos.

Today I obtained permission from Colton Lipka to begin sampling for blackfish. He requested that I cull all blackfish that I do capture.

We received a call reporting that the loon on Johnson Lake now has a piece of wood attached to it by fishing line, so I grabbed a solo canoe and Todd's truck and drove down there. I was able to capture the loon, cut the fishing line from it, and release it. It and another loon had been out in the lake with a couple of tiny chicks. I was only able to capture the loon because it stayed with the chicks, allowing me to get close. The one loon had apparently swallowed a lure or hook, which I could not see or pull out, so I just cut the fishing line at the bird's bill. The loon was robust, lively, and apparently healthy when released.
